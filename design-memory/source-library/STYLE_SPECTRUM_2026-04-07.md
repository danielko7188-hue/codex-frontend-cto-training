## How To Use It

1. Pick one primary lane.
2. Optionally pick one support lane.
3. Pick one anti-lane to avoid drift.
4. Use 2-4 references from that lane, not ten.
5. Build the page around the lane's actual logic, not just its colors.

## Why This Exists

The old failure mode was too much dependence on one aesthetic:

- calm
- premium
- restrained
- tasteful

That is useful, but not enough.

Good design flexibility means knowing when the product should feel:

- operational
- industrial
- warm
- editorial
- launch-driven
- dense and efficient
- AI-native but controlled

## Selection Heuristics

Choose `minimalist-premium` when:

- brand trust and calm polish matter most
- the product is modern and high-end

Choose `enterprise-trust` when:

- the product sells operational reliability, compliance, or workflow clarity

Choose `editorial-luxury` when:

- brand, storytelling, and perception matter as much as function

Choose `technical-industrial` when:

- the product is manufacturing, infrastructure, B2B tooling, or systems-heavy

Choose `warm-service` when:

- the business is service-led and trust should feel human, not corporate-cold

Choose `bold-launch` when:

- attention and momentum matter more than restraint

Choose `calm-ai-workspace` when:

- the product is AI-assisted work and needs clarity, trust, and inspectable output

Choose `operator-density` when:

- power users need more information per screen and less decorative breathing room

## Anti-Drift Rule

If the page starts to look like a generic startup template:

1. stop
2. restate the lane
3. remove the lowest-value components
4. strengthen the dominant visual idea
